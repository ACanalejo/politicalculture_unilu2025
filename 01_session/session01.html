Why culture?


Traditional approaches to political behavior has focused on:

  1. Institutions and rationality (rational choice)

  2. Socio-demographic factors (sociological approach)

  3. Psychology and cognition (psychological approach)

Culture, despite admittedly pervasive, has been largely ignored in empirical political science until recently

The stronger presence of culture in political science

  1. New methods (i.e., identification revolution and machine learning)

  2. More data (i.e., digitalization of large corpora of text, including archival data)

The WEIRD realization


Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic (WEIRD) societies

  • Replication crisis in psychology and the social sciences unveiled

  • Influential paper: Henrich et al (2010)

What WEIRD differences do Henrich et al find?

  • Visual perception (large vs small scale societies)

  • Fairness and cooperation in decision-making (large vs small scale societies)

  • Anti-social punishment (Western vs “non-western” societies)

  • Individualism (contemporary US vs. the rest of the world)

Etc.

Teaching policy

  • This course is a mixed between a seminar and a lecture

  • Thus, I expect you to have read the mandatory readings and to participate

  • I will introduce the topic and guide the discussion, but you should actively pursue the debate

  • The main drivers of the debate will be the readings, but we can (will) deviate from them, the goal is to develop critical thinking!

Course requirements

  • Only English as a formal requirements

  • Knowledge of quantitative methods is recommended

  • Knowledge of political science is an asset

Integration and interaction policy


  • Equal participation will be actively promoted

  • Please use gender neutral language whenever possible

  • Discrimination or bullying of any type is strictly forbidden

Artificial Intelligence (AI) policy


  • AI use is permitted

  • However, AI must augment, not substitute humans

  • Obligation to declare AI use for every assignment (i.e., cheating will be punished)

Evaluation I

  1. Attendance

    • Max. two missing sessions

  2. Mandatory readings

    • Reading questions (not evaluated)

    • Additional resources in the syllabus

  3. Active participation

    • “No such thing as a stupid question”

Evaluation II

  1. Two response papers

    • Not a summary!

    • 500-700 words

    • One for block II and one for block III

    • Deadline: previous Friday at noon (12:00)

    • More information in the syllabus

Evaluation III

  1. Research proposal

    • Research paper without results, but it must be feasible

    • 2500-3000 words

    • Deadline: May 2nd at noon (12:00)

    • Two additional not for evaluation deadlines: “Research question” (March 20th) and “Research proposal outline (April 10th)”

    • More information in the syllabus and in the PolSem guidelines for research

Evaluation IV

  1. Research proposal presentation

    • 15 minutes presentation + 15 minutes of discussion and Q&A (flexibility depending on registered members)

    • Incorporating feedback to the research proposal

    • During Block IV

  2. Discussion

    • One discussant for presenter

    • It must be prepared in advance and includes constructive feedback (e.g., sandwhich technique)
