Can Policy Change Culture?

(Bau, 2021)


Three traditional kinship models

  1. Matrilocal/matrilineal

  2. Patrilocal/patrilineal

  3. Neolocal

The argument


Matrilocal (patrilocal) practices serve to ensure that the daughter (son) which child will take care of the parents when they become old


This culture has implications for selective investment in imperfectly altruistic settings

The argument


The introduction of pension plans as a feature of modernization should remove the need for this investment, thus eliminating matrilocal/patrilocal practices


Therefore, a relatively small policy can have huge cultural implications

Empirical strategy


Matching local ethnicity census data with ethnic practices data (Ethnographic Atlas & Ethnologue)

The Effect of a Supreme Court Decision on Social Norms

(Tankard & Paluck, 2017)


Traditional research question

Do institutional decisions influence people’s attitudes?


This paper’s research question

Do institutional decisions influence perceptions of social norms?


Social norms ≠ personal attitudes


Both are argued to have independent behavioral influence


However, the influence of institutional decisions on behavior via social norms have not been tested

The argument


The link between institutional decisions and attitudes is not clear (ideology, experience). However, institutional decisions may influence social norms because:

  1. They may signal group opinion (representation of a collective)

  2. They serve as an expert projection of the direction in which public opinion will move

  3. Simultaneous observation of the same decision

Obergefell v. Hodges 2015


US Supreme Court’s ruling allowing same sex marriage


Expectations

  1. It will increase perceived public support toward same sex marriage

  2. It will not increase private personal support

Empirical strategy


Study 1

Experiment before the Supreme Court ruling manipulating the perceived likelihood that the Court ruling will be positive or negative


Study 2

Panel survey around the time of the Supreme Court decision

What are the implications of this study?

“…can institutional decisions change society, even when they don’t change individual opinions?” (Schmid)

And what about its limitations?


“It would be desirable to examine whether repeated exposure to institutional messages or greater personalization of the decision could also generate changes in individual beliefs.” (Schmid)


“…the study is unable to evaluate whether the variation in perceived norms leads to concrete behaviors of greater inclusivity or public support.” (Schmid)

“Although the authors of the text try to isolate the effect of the decision itself, in reality it is difficult to separate what people learn from the Court from what they receive through media narration.” (Schmid)


“…the study does not adequately explore possible differences in effect between subgroups of the population, such as according to level of education, age or degree of religiosity, which could react differently to normative change.” (Schmid)


“Finally, it would be advisable to consider the potential impact of institutional decisions made by bodies perceived as less legitimate […] in contexts characterized by a high level of disaffection towards institutions, this mechanism may not be effective or may produce undesirable outcomes, such as polarization.” (Schmid)
